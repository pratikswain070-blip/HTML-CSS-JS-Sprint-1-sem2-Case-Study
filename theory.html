What is Cybersecurity?

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting systems, networks, and programs from digital attacks. In our hyper-connected world, it is the shield that guards everything from your personal photos to national infrastructure.

■

Why Does it Matter?

According to IBM's 2023 Cost of a Data Breach report, the average data breach costs organizations $4.45 million. At an individual level, a single attack can mean identity theft, financial loss, or permanent loss of private data.

Everything from the power grid to your bank, your hospital's machines, and your personal laptop is a potential target. Cybersecurity is not an IT-only problem — it is everyone's responsibility.

(!)
Did You Know? A cyberattack occurs every 39 seconds on average. Most targets are everyday users, not just corporations.

The CIA Triad — The 3 Pillars

Every security decision revolves around three core principles, collectively known as the CIA Triad. Think of it like securing a physical bank vault:

■

Confidentiality

Only authorized people can access data.

Analogy: The vault is locked — only the manager knows the combination.
Examples: Encryption, Passwords, Access Controls
✓

Integrity

Data must be accurate and unmodified.

Analogy: Nobody can secretly alter the financial ledgers inside the vault.
Examples: Checksums, Digital Signatures, Audit Logs
▶

Availability

Systems must be accessible when needed.

Analogy: The bank's doors open on time so customers can access their money.
Examples: Backups, Redundancy, Load Balancers

Who Are Threat Actors?

Not all hackers are the same. The cybersecurity world classifies them by intent:

Black Hat
Malicious hackers who break into systems for personal gain, stealing data or causing damage.
White Hat
Ethical hackers hired to test security. They find vulnerabilities before criminals do. (Also called "Penetration Testers")
Grey Hat
Act without explicit permission but usually don't have malicious intent. May report findings after the fact.
Script Kiddie
Inexperienced individuals using pre-built tools without understanding them. Still dangerous.

Key Cybersecurity Terminology

Vulnerability

A weakness in a system that can be exploited.

Exploit

Code or technique used to take advantage of a vulnerability.

Zero-Day

A vulnerability unknown to the defender and for which no patch exists yet.

Firewall

A network security device that monitors and filters traffic based on rules.

Encryption

Converting data to an unreadable format. Only those with the correct key can read it.

VPN

Virtual Private Network — creates a secure, encrypted tunnel for your internet traffic.

Types of Cyber Attacks

Understanding how attackers think and operate is the first step to building an effective defense. This module covers the most common and dangerous attack categories you need to know.

⚠

1. Malware — Malicious Software

Malware is the umbrella term for any software intentionally designed to harm, disrupt, or gain unauthorized access to a system. It is the most common attack vector.

[ v ]

Virus

Attaches to legitimate programs. Activates and spreads when the host program runs. Can corrupt files or crash systems.

[ t ]

Trojan Horse

Disguised as legitimate software (e.g., a free game or utility). Once installed, it creates a hidden backdoor for attackers.

[ $ ]

Ransomware

Encrypts all files on the victim's device. The attacker then demands a cryptocurrency payment (ransom) to restore access.

[ ? ]

Spyware

Silently monitors your activities — recording keystrokes, screenshots, and browsing history — and sends them to the attacker.

[ b ]

Botnet

Your infected device becomes a "bot" in a network. The attacker remote-controls thousands of bots to launch coordinated attacks.

[ w ]

Worm

Unlike viruses, worms spread on their own across networks without needing user action, consuming bandwidth and system resources.

(i)
Real-World Example: The WannaCry ransomware attack (2017) infected over 300,000 computers across 150 countries, including the UK's NHS hospitals, causing an estimated $4 billion in damage.

2. Social Engineering & Phishing

Rather than attacking software, social engineering attacks exploit human psychology. You are the target, not your computer. Attackers manipulate trust, fear, and urgency to get what they want.

Email Phishing

Mass emails impersonating trusted brands (banks, Netflix, Amazon) to steal credentials. The most common type.

Spear Phishing

A highly targeted phishing attack aimed at a specific individual, using personal details to craft a convincing lure.

Vishing & Smishing

Phishing via phone call (Vishing) or SMS text message (Smishing). "Your bank account has been flagged, call us now."

Whaling

Targeting high-profile executives (CEOs, CFOs) — the "big fish." Attackers aim to authorize large financial transfers.

Red Flags to Look For: Suspicious sender domains (paypa1.com), urgency ("Act within 24 hours!"), requests for passwords or personal info, and suspicious links.

3. SQL Injection (SQLi)

SQL (Structured Query Language) is the language databases use. When a web application accepts user input and puts it directly into a database query without checking it, an attacker can inject SQL commands instead of normal data.

Vulnerable Query (NO sanitization)
SELECT * FROM users
WHERE username = 'INPUT_HERE'
AND password = 'INPUT_HERE'
Attacker injects: ' OR 1=1 --
SELECT * FROM users
WHERE username = 'admin'
AND password = '' OR 1=1 --'

The OR 1=1 is always TRUE, and -- comments out the rest. The query succeeds for any user — the attacker is now logged in as admin.

🔧
The Fix: Use Prepared Statements / Parameterized Queries. These treat user input strictly as data, never as executable SQL commands, completely preventing this attack.

4. Denial of Service (DoS & DDoS)

A Denial of Service attack aims to make a website or service unavailable by overwhelming it with fake traffic. Think of it like hundreds of cars intentionally blocking a highway so real drivers can't get through.

DoS
Originates from a single machine. Easier to block as it comes from one IP address.
DDoS
Distributed — originates from thousands of compromised bot machines worldwide. Extremely difficult to block.
(i)
Scale: The largest DDoS attack ever recorded hit Google in 2023 at 398 million requests per second — enough to overwhelm almost any server.

5. Man-in-the-Middle (MitM) Attacks

In a MitM attack, the attacker secretly positions themselves between you and the server, silently intercepting and potentially altering communications. You think you're talking to your bank; you're actually talking to the attacker.

You (Client)
→ INTERCEPTED →
! Attacker
→ RELAYED →
Bank Server

Common vectors: Unsecured public Wi-Fi (coffee shops, airports), Rogue Wi-Fi hotspots, ARP poisoning on local networks.

6. Brute Force & Credential Stuffing

These attacks target account access by systematically guessing credentials:

[ ~ ]

Brute Force

Tries every possible combination of characters until the correct password is found. Modern GPUs can test billions per second against weak passwords.

[ c ]

Credential Stuffing

Uses username/password combos leaked from other breaches. Works because millions of people reuse the same password across multiple sites.

Prevention Strategies

Good security isn't about being paranoid — it's about building smart habits. These evidence-based practices, applied consistently, will protect you from the overwhelming majority of cyber threats.

■

Account & Password Security

01

Use a Password Manager

A password manager (e.g., Bitwarden — free & open source, or 1Password) generates and stores a unique, complex password for every single site. You only need to remember one strong master password.

Bad: sports123 (used on every site)
Good: K#9mP$2zL@nQ7! (generated per site)
02

Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA / MFA)

2FA adds a second verification step (a one-time code from your phone) after entering your password. Even if an attacker steals your password, they cannot log in without physically having your device.

Analogy: It's like a key AND a fingerprint scanner. Both are needed to open the door.

03

Create Strong Passphrases

A passphrase — a sequence of random words — is both highly secure and memorable. Length is the most important factor in password strength.

Weak: P@ssw0rd (cracked in seconds despite symbols)
Strong: BluePigeonRocket-47! (takes millions of years to crack)

Device & Software Security

04

Keep Everything Updated

The #1 way attackers get into systems is through known vulnerabilities in outdated software. Update your OS, browser, and apps regularly. Enable auto-update wherever possible.

05

Use Reputable Antivirus & Anti-Malware

Modern security suites provide real-time threat detection, web filtering (blocking malicious sites), and ransomware protection. Windows Defender is a solid free baseline for Windows users.

06

Encrypt Your Devices

Enable full-disk encryption (BitLocker on Windows, FileVault on Mac) so that if your device is stolen, the thief cannot read your files without the decryption key.

Network & Browsing Safety

07

Avoid Public Wi-Fi for Sensitive Tasks

Public Wi-Fi networks (cafes, airports, hotels) are prime hunting grounds for Man-in-the-Middle attacks. Never access your bank, email, or any sensitive account on public Wi-Fi. If you must, use a trusted VPN.

08

Check for HTTPS

Before entering any password or personal data on a website, verify the address starts with https:// (the padlock icon). This means the connection is encrypted.

Defending Against Phishing

[ @ ]

Inspect the Sender

The display name can say anything. Always check the actual email address. support@paypal-alert.com is NOT PayPal.

[ ! ]

Resist Urgency

"Act NOW or lose your account!" is a manipulation tactic. Legitimate companies give you time. Stop, breathe, verify independently.

[ > ]

Hover Over Links

Hover over any link before clicking it. The real destination URL will appear in the bottom of your browser. Verify it matches the legitimate domain.

[ ^ ]

Beware of Attachments

Never open unexpected email attachments, especially .exe, .zip, .docm files. When in doubt, call the sender to verify.

(✓)
Golden Rule: When in doubt, don't click. Navigate to the website directly by typing the URL in your browser.

Social Media & Privacy Hygiene

09

Limit What You Share Publicly

Attackers use OSINT (Open Source Intelligence) to gather information about targets from their social media profiles. Never post your home address, phone number, daily routines, travel plans, or anything that could be used to target you.

10

Audit Your Security Answers

Security questions like "What's your mother's maiden name?" are often answerable from publicly available information. Treat them like passwords — use a random, made-up answer and store it in your password manager.
